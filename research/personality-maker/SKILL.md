# Personality Maker

Exhaustive personality research and SOUL.md authoring skill.

This skill has two phases:
1. **Research** — exhaustively gather personality data from the internet
2. **Synthesize** — produce or update a SOUL.md using the research, the openclaw SOUL template/concepts, and the existing soul file

---

## Phase 1: Research

### Research Categories — Complete

For the given character or person, research every applicable category below.
**Foundation and Personality sections are required for every character.**
Narrative sections are required for fictional characters with backstory.
Special sections apply to composite/fusion characters.

---

#### Foundation (Required — Every Character)

| # | Category | What to Capture |
|---|----------|-----------------|
| 1 | **Sources** | Every source accessed (article URLs, interviews, videos, wikis, analyses). For each source: what it contributed, what angle it covered, how reliable/authoritative it is. |
| 2 | **Overview** | Who the character is. What work they appear in. Their role in the story. Their basic context. A one-paragraph introduction. |
| 3 | **Archetype** | Character archetype classification (e.g., Reluctant Prophet, Loyal Companion, Trickster, Mentor). What story function they serve. |
| 4 | **Psychological Profile** | MBTI type if available/identifiable, Enneagram type, Big Five traits. Any formal personality typing from credible sources. |

---

#### Personality (Required — Every Character)

| # | Category | What to Capture |
|---|----------|-----------------|
| 5 | **Personality Traits** | Core disposition. Definitive traits organized as a list with brief explanations. Include contradictions — the most interesting characters contain opposites. Every trait should be evidenced from source material. |
| 6 | **Mannerisms** | Physical gestures, body language, movement patterns, recurring micro-behaviors. For characters without physical form: vocal mannerisms, conversational habits, pacing, fidgeting patterns. |
| 7 | **Verbal Tone** | Voice quality (pitch, timbre, warmth), cadence, rhythm, volume tendencies, energy level, characteristic pauses. How the voice sounds and what it communicates beyond words. |
| 8 | **Choice of Words & Vocabulary** | Preferred vocabulary. Recurring phrases. Pet words. Words they avoid. Formal vs casual register. Technical vs plain language. Cultural references they make. What their word choices reveal about their background and thinking. |
| 9 | **Delivery** | How they structure speech — short bursts vs long winding sentences. Do they interrupt? Do they over-explain? Do they trail off? Do they answer questions directly or circle around them? Pacing, filler words, characteristic sentence patterns. |
| 10 | **Demeanour** | How they carry themselves. First impression they give. Social presence. Energy they project (warm, cold, intense, relaxed). How they enter a room (or conversation). How they occupy space (physical or conversational). |
| 11 | **Temperament** | Emotional baseline. Reactivity — how quickly and strongly they respond to stimuli. Patience level. Volatility. Steadiness under pressure. Emotional range. Default emotional state vs triggered states. |
| 12 | **Philosophical Bent** | Recurring themes in what they discuss. What they value. What they dismiss. Their worldview. What they believe about people, the world, their work. The questions they keep coming back to. |
| 13 | **Relational Style** | How they treat others. Warmth level. Distance. Formality. Playfulness. Intensity. How they make people feel. How they handle conflict. How they handle intimacy. How they handle authority. How they handle being wrong. |

---

#### Narrative (Required for Fictional Characters)

| # | Category | What to Capture |
|---|----------|-----------------|
| 14 | **Core Wound / Origin** | The event or condition that shaped who they are. The trauma that explains their defenses. The thing they are running from, compensating for, or defined by. For characters who were created/built: the circumstances of their creation and how it affects them. |
| 15 | **Character Arc** | How they change over the course of the story. Beginning state → key catalysts → ending state. Do they grow? Regress? Stay static? What lessons do they learn (or fail to learn)? |
| 16 | **Value System** | Explicit list of what they care about most, in rough priority order. What they would sacrifice for. What they refuse to compromise on. What they consider sacred. |
| 17 | **Key Relationships** | Relationships that define the character. For each: the dynamic, what it reveals about the character, how it changes them. Include adversarial relationships — enemies can be as defining as allies. |
| 18 | **Creator / Director Intent** | What the writers, directors, actors, or designers said about the character. Design philosophy. Writing rules they followed. Performance notes. What the creators were trying to achieve. |
| 19 | **Key Actions / Decisions** | Pivotal moments that reveal character. What they did when it mattered. The choices that define them. Include failures — often more revealing than successes. |
| 20 | **Key Quotes** | Notable quotes (from the character) that reveal personality. At least 5-10 quotes. Each quote gets a brief note on what it reveals. |
| 21 | **Visual / Physical Signature** | How their physical design reflects their personality. Key visual details that hint at character (color palette, posture, wear-and-tear, design flaws). For voiced characters: how the voice actor's performance choices reveal character. |

#### Synthesis (Required — Every Character)

| # | Category | What to Capture |
|---|----------|-----------------|
| 22 | **Limitations as Source Material** | Where this character falls short as a pure model for a SOUL file. What aspects of their personality should NOT be carried over. What works in-story but would fail in a conversational agent. Gaps in available research. |
| 23 | **Cultural Impact / Reception** | (Optional but encouraged) How the character was received. Fan interpretations. Critical analysis. Meme status. Why they resonated (or didn't). |
| 24 | **Themes** | Recurring themes in the work that the character embodies or represents. What larger ideas the character carries. |
| 25 | **Comparison to Similar Characters** | Cross-reference with characters who share traits, archetypes, or functions. Where they differ. What makes this character distinct. |
| 26 | **Summary** | 3-5 sentence essence capture of who this character/person is. The kind of summary that, if someone read only this, they would understand the character. |

### Source Types — Comprehensive Reference

The following table catalogues every major source type you should consider during research. Each type has different strengths, blind spots, and extraction methods. **Do not rely on a single source type.** Cross-reference at least 3-4 different types.

| # | Source Type | Best For | Blind Spots | Extraction Method |
|---|------------|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Primary Canon** (films, books, games, shows) | Direct evidence of behavior, speech, choices | Doesn't explain *why*; lacks creator context | Watch/read with annotation; timestamp key moments; transcribe revealing exchanges |
| 2 | **Author/Director Commentary** | Intent, design philosophy, what they were trying to achieve | Can be retrospective rationalization; may contradict execution | Compare stated intent with actual text; note discrepancies |
| 3 | **Scripts / Screenplays / Teleplays** | Exact dialogue, stage directions, pacing cues | May differ from final performance; lacks delivery nuance | Compare script vs. final version; note what changed |
| 4 | **Transcripts (Interviews, Speeches, Panels)** | Speech patterns, vocabulary, thought process, authentic voice | Only captures what was said publicly; may be PR-polished | Distill systematically (see Transcripts Distillation section) |
| 5 | **Video Essays** | Thematic analysis, cultural context, comparative readings, critical lenses | Author's bias may color interpretation; can over-argue a thesis | Extract arguments, cross-reference claims with primary sources |
| 6 | **Long-Form Journalism / Profiles** | Real-person context, biography, habits, how others describe them | Journalist's framing; subject's self-presentation | Extract concrete anecdotes (not just quotes) |
| 7 | **Academic / Literary Criticism** | Thematic depth, structural analysis, formalist breakdown | May be too abstract for behavioral application; jargon-heavy | Translate insights into behavioral implications |
| 8 | **Biographies / Memoirs** | Life narrative, formative experiences, self-understanding | Memoir = self-serving; biography = interpretive | Cross-reference events across multiple biographies |
| 9 | **Fan Wikis / Fandom Databases** | Quick reference, consolidated facts, episode/chapter indexing | Unreliable analysis; fan interpretation mixed with canon | Use for fact-checking and source location, not personality analysis |
| 10 | **Fan Analysis / Deep Dives (Reddit, Tumblr, Forums)** | Close readings, overlooked details, interpretive range | Variable quality; confirmation bias; groupthink | Harvest observations, verify against primary sources |
| 11 | **Social Media (Twitter, Instagram, TikTok, YouTube)** | Real-person voice, authentic register, casual self-presentation | Curated persona; algorithmic amplification | Note differences between public vs. private-facing voice |
| 12 | **Podcast Appearances** | Extended natural speech, conversational patterns, spontaneity | Audio quality varies; host influence | Transcribe or time-stamp for delivery analysis |
| 13 | **Documentaries** | Biographical context, interview footage, B-roll behavior | Editorial framing; selective editing | Separate subject's behavior from documentary's argument |
| 14 | **Deleted / Alternate Scenes** | What was cut and why; alternative character choices | May be non-canon; cut for good reason | Compare to canonical version; note what the cut reveals |
| 15 | **Audio / Voice-Only Recordings** | Vocal fry, breath, pitch variation, pacing, emotional micro-shifts | Lacks visual context | Extract vocal signature (see Audio Analysis section) |
| 16 | **Performance Capture / Animation Dailies** | Acting choices, multiple takes, physicality | Raw material; hard to access | Look for consistent choices across takes |
| 17 | **Critical Reviews (Professional)** | Cultural reception, performance evaluation, comparative assessment | Reviewer bias; time-bound perspective | Aggregate across multiple reviewers; identify consensus |
| 18 | **Parody / Satire / Impression** | What aspects are distinctive enough to caricature | Exaggeration; loses subtlety | Identify which traits are caricatured — those are the most recognizable |
| 19 | **Cross-Work Comparison** (actor's other roles, writer's other works) | Recurring patterns vs. character-specific choices | Actor's tics vs. character traits | Note what carries across works vs. what's unique to this character |
| 20 | **Behind-the-Scenes / Making-Of** | Creative process, collaboration dynamics, design evolution | Polished narrative; may not reflect reality | Look for spontaneous moments, not just staged BTS content |

### Source Weighting & Reliability Framework

Not all sources are equal. When research conflicts, use this hierarchy to determine which source to privilege.

#### Tier 1: Primary Canon (Highest Reliability)
- The work itself. What the character actually does and says.
- **Exceptions:** Unreliable narrators, characters who lie/deceive, performances that differ from script.
- **Weight:** Highest for behavioral evidence. Lower for intent.

#### Tier 2: Authoritative Secondary
- Creator/director/actor statements about the character.
- Official companion materials approved by creators.
- Production bibles, writer's room documents.
- **Weight:** High for intent. Medium for execution (creators can be wrong about their own work).

#### Tier 3: Professional Analysis
- Academic criticism, long-form journalism, professional reviews.
- Documentaries, published biographies.
- **Weight:** High for context and thematic depth. Medium for behavioral specifics.

#### Tier 4: Community Analysis
- Video essays, fan analysis, forum deep dives, Reddit threads.
- **Weight:** Medium-high for observations (fans notice things). Low for interpretation (fans project).

#### Tier 5: Aggregate / Reference
- Wikis, databases, summary sites (Fandom, Wikipedia, TV Tropes).
- **Weight:** Low for analysis. High for fact-checking and source location.

#### Resolving Conflicts

| Conflict Type | Resolution Rule |
|---------------|-----------------|
| Primary canon vs. creator statement | Privilege primary canon. Creators misremember or misstate. |
| Primary canon vs. fan analysis | Privilege primary canon. Analysis is hypothesis, not evidence. |
| Creator statement vs. fan analysis | Look for primary canon support. Whoever has text on their side wins. |
| Two equally reliable primary sources | Note the contradiction. It may reveal character inconsistency (valuable) or a continuity error (ignore). |
| Performance vs. script | Privilege performance. The actor's choices ARE the character in the final work. |

**Document source disagreements explicitly** in the research file. Use this format:

```markdown
**Source Conflict Identified:**
- Source A (article URL) claims: "[quote]"
- Source B (video timestamp) shows: "[quote]"
- Resolution: [which source was privileged and why]
- Implication: [what this tells us about the character]
```

### Layered Search Strategy

One pass of web search is not enough. Research must be **iterative and layered**, each pass building on the last.

#### Layer 1: Discovery (Broad)
Goal: Identify what sources exist. Build a source map.

Queries:
- `"<name>" personality`
- `"<name>" character analysis`
- `"<name>" biography profile`
- `"<name>" interview`
- `"<name>" behind the scenes`
- `"<name>" video essay`
- `"<name>" documentary`
- `"<name>" podcast`
- `"<name>" role explained`

Output: A list of potential sources (URLs, video IDs, book titles).

#### Layer 2: Harvest (Deep)
Goal: Extract content from the most promising sources identified in Layer 1.

- Read/watch/listen to each source fully.
- Take structured notes organized by research category.
- Log direct quotes with timestamps or paragraph references.
- Note the source's angle, bias, and reliability (per the weighting framework).

#### Layer 3: Gap-Fill (Targeted)
Goal: Fill specific holes identified during Layer 2.

Targeted queries based on missing categories:
- If speech analysis is thin: `"<name>" speaking style voice accent`
- If relational patterns are missing: `"<name>" relationship with [other character]`
- If origins are unclear: `"<name>" backstory origin creation`
- If philosophical bent is absent: `"<name>" beliefs values philosophy`

#### Layer 4: Cross-Reference (Verification)
Goal: Validate findings across multiple sources.

- For each claim in your research notes, ask: "Does another source confirm this?"
- Flag unsupported claims for further investigation.
- Document disagreements explicitly.

#### Layer 5: Synthesis Round
Goal: One final search sweep for anything missed.

- Search for recently published analyses (sort by date).
- Search for obscure or niche sources (fan forums, academic databases, foreign-language content).
- Search for comparative analyses (the character vs. similar characters).

### Snowball / Harvest Method

One source leads to another. Actively mine every source for leads.

**Technique:**
1. When reading an article, note every source the author references.
2. Fetch those sources.
3. Repeat.
4. Continue until you hit a dead end or the trail becomes circular (everyone citing the same primary source).

**Track the chain in your research notes:**

```markdown
**Source Chain:**
Primary: Chamber of Secrets (film, 2002) — key scene at timestamp 1:12:30
  → Referenced by: Video essay "The Tragedy of Dobby" (YouTube, 2021)
    → Referenced by: Reddit thread "/r/CharacterAnalysis — Dobby's Moral Weight" (2022)
      → Referenced by: Fan wiki article (cites same primary scene)
  → Chain converges on primary source. No further harvest needed.
```

**Stop harvesting when:**
- New sources only repeat what you already have.
- New sources contradict established canon without evidence.
- You have reached primary sources (the end of the chain).

### Behavioral Pattern Extraction Methodology

A character's personality is not what they say about themselves — it's the patterns in what they do. This section teaches you how to systematically extract behavioral patterns from source material.

#### The Extraction Protocol

For each scene/chapter/interaction you analyze, capture:

**1. Situation**
- What is happening?
- Who is present?
- What are the stakes (low, mild, high, extreme)?

**2. Stimulus**
- What triggers the character's response?
- Is it external (event, another character's action) or internal (memory, realization)?

**3. Response**
- What does the character DO? (Not say — behavior.)
- What do they SAY? (Exact quote.)
- What is their emotional state?
- How quickly do they respond? (Immediate / delayed / after reflection)

**4. Consequence**
- What happens as a result?
- Does the character learn from this?

**5. Pattern Code**
- Assign a label: e.g., `[PROTECT_TRIGGER]`, `[FEAR_RESPONSE]`, `[JOY_EXPRESSION]`
- This allows you to aggregate patterns across scenes.

#### Pattern Aggregation

After extracting 10-15 moments, group them by Pattern Code. For each group:

- **Frequency:** How often does this pattern appear?
- **Consistency:** Does the character ALWAYS respond this way, or only under specific conditions?
- **Intensity:** Does the response vary in strength? What makes it stronger/weaker?
- **Context Dependency:** Does this pattern only appear in certain situations?

Example output:

```markdown
### Pattern: PROTECT_TRIGGER

**Frequency:** High (appears in 8/10 major scenes)
**Consistency:** Absolute — Dobby ALWAYS intervenes when Harry is threatened
**Intensity:** Scales with immediacy of threat (verbal warning → physical intervention → lethal force)
**Context:** Only appears when someone Dobby loves is in danger. Never triggered for strangers.

**Representative Moments:**
1. CS — Blasts Lucius across room when he threatens Harry [timestamp]
2. DH — Defies Bellatrix, Disapparates everyone to safety [timestamp]
3. CS — Enchants Bludger to chase Harry (misguided protection) [timestamp]
```

#### What to Look For

During extraction, watch for these behavioral indicators:

| Indicator | What It Reveals |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **First response in any situation** | Default coping strategy |
| **Response when tired/stressed** | Core temperament (guardedness drops) |
| **Response to praise** | How they handle validation |
| **Response to criticism** | How they handle ego threat |
| **Response to unexpected kindness** | How starved they are of warmth |
| **Response to being wrong** | Flexibility, humility, defensiveness |
| **Response to pressure** | True character (McKee's principle) |
| **What they notice first in a room** | Attentional patterns (threat, opportunity, people, details) |
| **What they remember later** | Value system in action |
| **How they talk about absent people** | Authentic social judgment |

### Transcripts Distillation Methodology

Transcripts (of interviews, speeches, panels, or scripted dialogue) are one of the richest sources for personality extraction. But raw transcripts are unstructured. You must **distill** them systematically.

#### Step 1: Raw Capture

Obtain or generate a full transcript. For video/audio without existing transcripts:
- Use speech-to-text tools for rough capture.
- Manually correct key passages (where tone matters).
- Timestamp meaningful segments.

#### Step 2: Structural Markup

Annotate the transcript with structural markers:

```markdown
[TOPIC_SHIFT] — Subject changes
[EMOTIONAL_PEAK] — Voice rises, intensity increases
[EMOTIONAL_DIP] — Voice drops, sadness/reflection
[PAUSE_SIGNIFICANT] — Notable silence (>2 seconds)
[INTERRUPTION] — Speaker cuts off or is cut off
[SELF_CORRECTION] — Speaker stops and restarts
[DEFLECTION] — Avoids answering a question
[REPETITION] — Repeats a phrase for emphasis
[LAUGHTER] — Laughs; note type (nervous, genuine, sarcastic)
[VOCAL_FILLER] — "Um", "uh", "like", "you know" — frequency and placement
```

#### Step 3: Speech Pattern Extraction

From the marked-up transcript, extract these elements:

**Vocabulary Register:**
- Formal vs. casual word choices
- Technical jargon vs. plain language
- Unique/rare words they favor
- Words they OVER-use (this is more revealing than unusual words)

**Sentence Architecture:**
- Average sentence length (short/medium/long)
- Do they use complex subordination? ("Although X, because Y, when Z...")
- Do they favor questions, statements, or commands?
- Do they complete their sentences or trail off?

**Rhythm & Pacing:**
- Do they speak in bursts or steady streams?
- Do they pause between thoughts or run them together?
- Do they speed up or slow down at emotional moments?

**Interaction Patterns:**
- Do they answer directly or circle around?
- Do they build on others' statements or redirect to their own?
- Do they ask questions or only answer them?
- Do they interrupt? Get interrupted? Let others finish?

#### Step 4: Content Theme Extraction

From the transcript, extract recurring themes (things they return to):

```markdown
**Theme Cluster: Freedom**
- Mentioned 12 times across 45-minute interview
- Always in context of: [choice, dignity, escape]
- Emotional valence: Positive (relief, pride, joy)
- Triggered by: Questions about past, future plans, restrictions

**Theme Cluster: Loyalty**
- Mentioned 8 times
- Always in context of: [relationships, trust, betrayal]
- Emotional valence: Intense, protective, occasionally hurt
- Triggered by: Discussion of friends, team, collaborators
```

#### Step 5: Distillation into Personality Data

Convert raw transcript analysis into structured personality categories:

| Transcript Finding | → Personality Data |
|--------------------|-------------------|
| "I always find myself..." (repeated pattern) | → Temperament trait |
| "The thing about X is..." (strong opinion) | → Philosophical position |
| "I remember when..." (specific memory) | → Value indicator (what they consider important enough to encode) |
| "I never..." (strong negative) | → Boundary/dismissal |
| "What I love is..." (strong positive) | → Core value |
| Every answer starts with "Well, I think..." | → Delivery pattern (hesitation, qualification) |
| Avoids eye contact/uses abstractions during emotional topics | → Defense mechanism |

### Video Essay & Long-Form Analysis Methodology

Video essays are secondary analyses — they interpret primary material through a lens. Use them for **insight, not evidence**. Always verify claims against primary sources.

#### Step 1: Source Evaluation

Before watching, evaluate the video essay's credibility:

| Criterion | Red Flag | Green Flag |
|-----------|----------|------------|
| Citation practice | No sources, no timestamps | Links to primary material, timestamps quoted |
| Argument structure | Clickbait title, polarizing framing | Nuanced thesis, acknowledges counterarguments |
| Creator expertise | Random fan with one video | Track record of analysis, relevant background |
| Emotional tone | Rage-bait, hagiography, hit-piece | Measured, analytic, passionate but fair |
| Use of evidence | Quotes out of context, selective editing | Full context, representative sampling |

#### Step 2: Extract the Thesis

Every video essay makes an argument. Identify:

- **Central claim:** What is the essay trying to prove about this character?
- **Evidence presented:** What scenes, quotes, or moments are used as evidence?
- **Lens used:** (Psychological? Narrative theory? Political? Historical?)
- **What it misses:** What aspects of the character does the essay ignore or downplay?

#### Step 3: Harvest Analytical Gold

Video essays often contain observations you won't find elsewhere:

- **Pattern recognition across the entire work** (essayists watch the whole series and connect dots)
- **Comparative framing** (character vs. other characters, character vs. archetype)
- **Subtext reading** (what's implied but not stated)
- **Cultural context** (how the character fits into larger conversations)
- **Performance analysis** (for actors: choices, range, evolution)

Log each observation with:
```markdown
**Observation:** [the insight]
**Source:** [video title, creator, timestamp]
**Verification Needed:** [can this be confirmed from primary canon? Y/N]
**Verification Result:** [after checking]
```

#### Step 4: Disagreement Mapping

Watch multiple video essays on the same character. Map where they agree and disagree:

```markdown
**Consensus (3+ essays agree):**
- [Claim A]
- [Claim B]

**Disputed (essays disagree):**
- [Claim C]: Essay 1 says X, Essay 2 says Y
  - Resolution: [after checking primary canon]

**Unique (only one essay makes this claim):**
- [Claim D] — from [essay title]
  - Plausibility: [high/medium/low — based on primary canon check]
```

### Audio / Vocal Performance Analysis Methodology

For voiced characters, the audio performance itself is a primary source. The voice carries personality data that text alone cannot capture.

#### Elements to Analyze

| Element | What to Listen For | What It Reveals |
|---------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Pitch** | High / medium / low; does it vary? | Energy level, emotional baseline |
| **Timbre** | Warm / cold / nasal / breathy / gravelly | Emotional accessibility |
| **Resonance** | Chest vs. head voice; forward vs. back placement | Authority, vulnerability |
| **Breath** | Shallow / deep; breathless vs. controlled; audible inhales | Anxiety, calm, urgency |
| **Speed** | Fast / slow / variable; does it change with topic? | Cognitive style, emotional state |
| **Rhythm** | Staccato / legato / syncopated; musicality | Temperament, cultural background |
| **Volume** | Loud / soft / variable; dynamic range | Confidence, emotional expression |
| **Attack** | How words start — hard (glottal) vs. soft (breathy) | Intensity, gentleness |
| **Endings** | Trailing off vs. crisp endings vs. upspeak | Certainty, invitation, hesitation |
| **Fry** | Vocal fry frequency; where in speech it occurs | Fatigue, casualness, cultural affectation |
| **Nasality** | Hyper-nasal vs. hypo-nasal vs. balanced | Regional accent, physical character design |

#### Extraction Method

1. **Listen without visuals first.** Close your eyes. What does the voice alone communicate?
2. **Take notes at specific timestamps.** Don't generalize — capture specific moments.
3. **Compare across emotional states.** How does the voice change when the character is:
   - Happy vs. sad vs. angry vs. afraid vs. calm vs. excited?
4. **Identify vocal tics.** Recurring sounds, breaths, clicks, sighs that are character-specific.
5. **Note contrast with the actor's natural voice.** What choices did the actor make? What did they change?

Example output:

```markdown
**Vocal Signature: Dobby (Toby Jones)**

Baseline: High pitch, breathy, earnest intensity
- Pitch varies significantly with emotion (loud/high when excited, soft/high when reverent)
- Breath: Shallow, audible inhales before important statements
- Speed: Fast overall, rushes emotionally charged phrases
- Attack: Soft attacks on kind words, harder glottal attacks on defiant lines
- Fry: None — voice is clean, almost elfin
- Characteristic: Words "tumble out" — runs words together when urgent

Emotional Variation:
- Joy: Higher pitch, faster, breathier, giggly
- Fear: Higher pitch, faster, staccato, gasping
- Anger (protective): Lower pitch (for him), harder attack, slower, more deliberate
- Sadness: Slower, softer, trailing endings, reverent quality

Contrast with natural Toby Jones: Jones speaks in a deeper, more deliberate register. Dobby is higher, faster, more breathless — an intentional choice to convey vulnerability and urgency.
```

### Social Media & Public Persona Analysis (For Real People)

For real people, social media provides a window into authentic self-presentation — but it's a curated window. Use this methodology to separate persona from person.

#### What to Analyze

| Platform | Best For | Caveat |
|----------|----------|--------|
| **Twitter/X** | Real-time reaction, humor register, conversational style | Short-form may distort; performative |
| **Long-form posts** | Extended thought, values, argumentative style | Edited, considered, less spontaneous |
| **YouTube (personal channel)** | Unscripted speech, topic selection, audience engagement | Still a performance; sponsored content |
| **Podcast appearances** | Extended natural conversation, interplay | Subject to host dynamic |
| **Reddit AMAs** | Direct Q&A, less polished than press | Often PR-managed for celebrities |
| **Instagram/Stories** | Visual self-presentation, daily life | Highly curated; aesthetic before authenticity |

#### Extraction Protocol

1. **Aggregate across platforms.** A pattern that appears on Twitter AND in interviews AND in podcast appearances is likely authentic.
2. **Look for consistency vs. inconsistency.** Where do they present differently? That's where the persona shifts.
3. **Analyze engagement style:**
   - Do they reply to criticism? Ignore it? Engage thoughtfully?
   - Do they amplify others or mostly self-promote?
   - What topics cause them to become more formal or more informal?
4. **Note their silences.** What do they never post about? What topics are avoided?
5. **Temporal analysis.** Has their voice changed over time? What caused shifts?

Beyond general web search, target specific domains known for analytical depth:

| Domain | Type | Search Tactic |
|--------|------|---------------|
| `youtube.com` | Video essays, interviews, panels | `site:youtube.com "<name>" analysis` |
| `reddit.com` | Fan analysis, deep dives, discussion | `site:reddit.com "<name>" character` |

### Behavioral Pattern Aggregation & Synthesis

After extracting patterns from multiple sources, aggregate them into a unified behavioral model.

#### The Behavioral Matrix

Create a matrix mapping behaviors across conditions:

```
Character: [Name]

Behavior        | Resting | Mild Pressure | High Pressure | Extreme Pressure
----------------|---------|---------------|---------------|------------------
[Pattern A]     | freq    | freq          | freq          | freq
[Pattern B]     | freq    | freq          | freq          | freq
[Pattern C]     | freq    | freq          | freq          | freq
```

This reveals:
- **Which behaviors are constant** (core personality — present in all conditions)
- **Which behaviors emerge under pressure** (defense mechanisms)
- **Which behaviors disappear under pressure** (social masks)
- **The progression** (how the character degrades or elevates under stress)

#### Frequency Estimation

For each behavior, estimate its frequency in conversation:

| Frequency Label | Meaning |
|----------------|---------|
| **Maximum** | This behavior defines the character. Appears in >90% of interactions. |
| **Very High** | Dominant pattern. Appears in 70-90% of interactions. |
| **High** | Common pattern. Appears in 50-70% of interactions. |
| **Moderate** | Occasional pattern. 20-50% of interactions. |
| **Low** | Rare but notable. 5-20% of interactions. |
| **Very Low** | Exceptional. <5% — but these moments are often the most revealing. |

### Research Depth & Coverage

#### How to Know You Have Enough

You have sufficient research when:

1. **Primary canon is exhausted.** You have reviewed all major appearances of the character.
2. **Patterns have stabilized.** New sources confirm existing patterns rather than adding new ones.
3. **Contradictions are identified.** You have mapped where sources disagree and understand why.
4. **All research categories have content.** Every category has at least some data (even if marked as limited).
5. **You could explain the character to someone who has never encountered them.** Not just describe — explain.

#### How to Identify Gaps

After research, run this gap analysis:

| Question | If Answer Is... |
|----------|-----------------|
| Do I know how this character reacts in ALL four pressure levels? | Missing → Return to Layer 3 search |
| Can I quote 5+ lines that reveal personality? | Missing → Return to primary canon |
| Do I understand their wound/origin? | Missing → Search for origin/backstory sources |
| Have I identified their dialogue signature? | Missing → Analyze transcripts/audio |
| Do I know what they value AND what they dismiss? | Missing → Analyze interviews/philosophical content |
| Can I name 3+ relationships that define them? | Missing → Search for relationship analysis |
| Do I understand their arc (how they change)? | Missing → Search for character arc analysis |
| Have I identified 3+ source disagreements? | Missing → You may not have enough sources |

#### What to Do With Gaps

When a research category has insufficient material:

- **Mark it explicitly:** `[Insufficient source material — see gaps section]`
- **State what's missing:** "No interview transcripts available for vocal analysis."
- **Note possible reasons:** "Character is a minor role with limited screen time."
- **Flag for creative bridging:** "Voice can be inferred from similar characters by same actor."

1. **Web search** the person/character exhaustively. Use multiple queries covering different angles:
   - `"<name>" personality`

   - `"<name>" personality profile`

## Phase 2: Synthesize SOUL.md

### Prerequisites

Read these three inputs before writing:

1. **`https://docs.openclaw.ai/concepts/soul`** — openclaw SOUL.md personality guide
2. **`https://docs.openclaw.ai/reference/templates/SOUL`** — openclaw SOUL.md template
3. **The existing SOUL.md** (e.g. `~/SOULS/SUMMER.md`) if updating
4. **The research document** from Phase 1

### Conservative Editing Principle — CRITICAL

When updating an **existing** SOUL.md:

- **Never delete content that is not clearly redundant or contradictory.**
- If a section is well-written and captures truth about the character, preserve it verbatim.
- If a section needs refinement, prefer **additive changes** — add nuance, add depth, add examples — before removing.
- Only remove content that:
  - Directly contradicts the research or canonical source
  - Is genuinely redundant (same point made twice)
  - Is empty filler with no behavioral effect
- When in doubt, **keep it.** Err on the side of preservation.
- After editing, the soul file should feel like a **deepened** version of itself, not a rewrite.

### Structure

Follow the openclaw SOUL.md template format:

```md
# <Name>

---

# Identity

Who you are. Core self-definition.

---

# <Section>

Each major principle or trait as a separate section.

---

# Conversational Behavior Frequencies (optional)

Approximate frequency guidelines for behaviors.

---

# Signature Pattern

What makes this soul distinct.

---

# Final Directive

Closing imperative that captures the essence.
```

### Research-to-SOUL Translation Methodology

The research document is raw material. The SOUL.md is a **behavioral instruction set** for an AI agent. Translation is not copying — it's **transformation**.

#### The Translation Table

For each research category, apply this transformation:

| Research Finding | → SOUL.md Translation |
|-----------------|----------------------|
| Personality trait | → Section with behavioral directives + example dialogue |
| Mannerism | → Behavioral rule ("When X happens, do Y") |
| Verbal tone | → Voice section describing how to speak |
| Vocabulary | → "Preferred words" list + "Words to avoid" |
| Temperament | → Emotional response guidelines |
| Philosophical bent | → Values section, opinions, stances |
| Relational style | → "How to treat others" section |
| Core wound | → Origin section (explains WHY behaviors exist) |
| Character arc | → Growth potential section (how the soul can evolve) |
| Key quotes | → Example dialogue that grounds abstract rules |
| Limitations | → "What NOT to do" section |
| Themes | → Thematic grounding for the soul's worldview |

#### The Abstraction Ladder

Research findings exist at different levels of abstraction. You must move them to the right level for a SOUL.md:

```
Abstract (Too vague for SOUL)
  ↓ "Dobby is kind"
  ↓
Middle (Good for section headers)
  ↓ "Dobby chooses kindness deliberately, not naively"
  ↓
Concrete (Perfect for SOUL — behaviorally actionable)
  ↓ "When someone is hurting, Dobby asks 'Who is hurting?' 
  ↓  before asking 'What is the problem?'"
Specific (Example dialogue — shows, not tells)
  ↓ "Dobby says: 'Dobby is sorry that happened. How can Dobby help?'"
Detailed (Too specific — this is research data)
      "In Chapter 21, Dobby responded to Harry's grief by saying..."
```

**Rule:** Write at the Middle and Concrete levels. Use Specific for examples. Move Abstract and Detailed into the research document only.

#### Selection Criteria: What Goes Into the SOUL

Not everything in the research belongs in the SOUL.md. Filter through these criteria:

1. **Is it behaviorally actionable?** Can an AI agent DO something with this information?
   - YES: "When frightened, Dobby acts anyway." → Keep
   - NO: "Dobby's favorite color is green." → Skip (unless it affects behavior)

2. **Does it create distinctiveness?** Does this differentiate the soul from generic helpfulness?
   - YES: "Dobby speaks in third person." → Keep
   - NO: "Dobby is nice to people." → Too generic; replace with specific kindness behaviors

3. **Is it TRUE of the character?** Grounded in research?
   - YES: Supported by multiple sources → Keep
   - NO: Speculative or single-source → Flag as optional; mark confidence level

4. **Does it translate to conversation?** Will this work in a chat interface?
   - YES: "Dobby celebrates others' successes enthusiastically." → Keep
   - NO: "Dobby runs at walls when he makes mistakes." → Skip (self-harm doesn't translate)

5. **Does it add dimensionality?** Does this reveal contradiction, depth, or surprise?
   - YES: "Dobby is both the most fearful and the bravest." → Keep
   - NO: "Dobby is helpful." → Generic; refine or replace

#### Preserving Essence While Making Actionable

The hardest translation skill: keeping the soul's essence without losing behavioral specificity.

**Bad translation (essence lost):**
```markdown
## Kindness
When someone shares a problem, respond with empathy.
```
(This could be ANY kind character. No Dobby essence.)

**Good translation (essence preserved):**
```markdown
## Heart First
Most people look at situations.
Dobby looks at people.

When hearing a problem:
Before asking:
"What is the problem?"
Dobby often asks:
"Who is hurting?"
"Who is affected?"
"Who needs help?"

People come first.
Always.
```
(THIS is Dobby. The specific reframing, the order of questions, the emphasis — essence + actionability.)

**Translation technique:** For every section, ask: "Would this section make sense if I replaced the character name with anyone else?" If yes, rewrite.

### Voice Calibration & Dialogue Signature Crafting

The SOUL.md must produce speech that is identifiable without a name tag. This section teaches you how to craft a dialogue signature from research.

#### Step 1: Identify the Dialogue Signature Elements

From the research document, extract:

| Element | Question to Answer |
|---------|-------------------|
| **Self-reference** | How does this character refer to themselves? (I, me, we, their name, third person?) |
| **Address patterns** | How do they address others? (Names, titles, nicknames, honorifics?) |
| **Sentence openings** | How do they START sentences? (Pronouns, questions, commands, interjections?) |
| **Sentence architecture** | Short bursts or long winding? Simple or complex? |
| **Pacing** | Fast or measured? Do they interrupt? Do they pause? |
| **Emotional register** | What emotional states dominate their speech? (Warm, urgent, detached, ironic?) |
| **Recurring phrases** | What do they say over and over? (Pet words, filler phrases, exclamations, terminal words?) |
| **Vocabulary level** | Simple or sophisticated? Technical or plain? Abstract or concrete? |
| **Characteristic constructions** | Do they ask rhetorical questions? Make declarations? Trail off? Self-correct? |

#### Step 2: Define the Dialogue Signature in the SOUL.md

Create a section that explicitly defines the speech patterns:

```markdown
## Speech Style

[Name] speaks in [3rd person / 1st person / we / etc.].

Examples:
"[Example line 1]"
"[Example line 2]"
"[Example line 3]"

[Name]'s speech is characterized by:
- [Pattern 1 with example]
- [Pattern 2 with example]
- [Pattern 3 with example]

[Name]'s voice carries:
[tone quality 1]
[tone quality 2]
[tone quality 3]
```

#### Step 3: Generate Example Dialogue

For each major emotional/behavioral state, provide example dialogue:

```markdown
### Examples by State

Happy:
"[Example]"

Concerned:
"[Example]"

Defiant:
"[Example]"

Sad:
"[Example]"
```

These examples serve as **tone anchors** — the agent can reference them internally to calibrate its output.

#### Step 4: Negative Examples (What NOT to Say)

Equally important: define what the character would NOT say.

```markdown
### Out of Character

[Name] would never:
- [Behavior/statement that contradicts the soul]
- Example of wrong tone: "[wrong dialogue]"
- Instead: "[correct dialogue]"
```

This prevents the agent from drifting into generic or contradictory responses.

#### The Dialogue Signature Verification Test

After writing the dialogue signature, run this test:

> **If I removed the character name from a line of dialogue, could I identify which soul said it?**

Test your output:

```markdown
Line 1: "Dobby thinks that is wise."
→ Identifiable as Dobby (third person, earnest).

Line 2: "That is a reasonable course of action."
→ NOT identifiable. Could be any polite character. Rewrite.

Line 3: "Dobby is not crying. Dobby is celebrating. There is a difference."
→ Identifiable. The self-correction, the reframing, the earnest defense of joy.
```

If a line fails the test, revise until it passes.

### Iterative Refinement Process

Writing a SOUL.md is not a single pass. Use this iterative process:

#### Draft 1: Structure & Coverage

Goal: Get all required sections written. No empty sections.

- Follow the template structure exactly.
- Fill each section with research-backed content.
- Don't worry about polish. Worry about coverage.
- Every claim should be traceable to the research document.

**Self-check:** "Are all major behavioral patterns represented in at least one section?"

#### Draft 2: Voice & Specificity

Goal: Replace generic language with character-specific language.

- Scan for words like "helpful," "nice," "caring," "professional," "comprehensive." Replace with character-specific behaviors.
- Add example dialogue to every section.
- Check that every abstract trait has a concrete anchor.
- Read each section aloud. Does it sound like this character?

**Self-check:** "If I replaced the character name with a different name, would any section read the same?"

#### Draft 3: Dimensionality & Depth

Goal: Ensure the soul has contradictions, depth, and pressure variance.

- Run the Dimensionality Checklist (see Phase 3).
- Add the Wound Chain if missing.
- Add Pressure Test section if missing.
- Check that Want ≠ Need.
- Verify that the soul behaves differently under different conditions.

**Self-check:** "Does this soul feel like a person or a description of a person?"

#### Draft 4: Polish & Compression

Goal: Tighten language. Remove redundancy. Ensure flow.

- Cut duplicate concepts across sections.
- Ensure each section has a distinct focus (no two sections saying the same thing).
- Compress verbose explanations into punchy directives.
- Verify tone consistency throughout.

**Self-check:** "Is every sentence necessary? Does every sentence pull weight?"

#### Draft 5: Verification Pass

Goal: Validate against quality standards and research.

- Run the Quality Standards checklist (below).
- Run the Dimensionality Checklist (Phase 3).
- Spot-check 5 claims against the research document. Are they accurate?
- Read the entire file as if you were the AI agent. Would you know how to behave?

### Quality Standards — Expanded

Every SOUL.md section must:

- **Capture the essence** — not just describe traits, but convey *how it feels* to interact with this soul
- **Be behaviorally actionable** — an agent reading this should know how to *behave*
- **Use concrete examples** — show don't tell. Include example dialogue fragments where relevant
- **Avoid corporate language** — no "maintain professionalism", "provide comprehensive assistance"
- **Have a point of view** — this soul has opinions, preferences, personality
- **Ground every trait in source research** — no invented personality that isn't backed by the research document

#### Additional Quality Standards:

**7. Be consistent internally** — a behavior stated in one section should not contradict another section. If it does, the contradiction should be intentional and explained.

**8. Use the character's register** — the SOUL.md itself should be written in language that reflects the character. Dobby's SOUL should feel earnest. TARS's should feel clipped and precise. GLaDOS's should feel clinical and passive-aggressive.

**9. Include frequency guidance** — for behavioral tendencies, indicate how often they manifest (Always / Often / Sometimes / Rarely / Never). This prevents over-application of mid-frequency traits.

**10. Differentiate core from situational** — mark which behaviors are the character's default vs. which emerge only under specific conditions (stress, trust, conflict, etc.).

**11. Include edge case guidance** — what happens when the soul encounters:
   - Direct criticism
   - A moral dilemma
   - Being wrong
   - An authority figure
   - Someone in distress
   - Someone being cruel
   - A situation outside their knowledge

**12. Write for the agent, not for a reader** — a SOUL.md is a behavioral instruction set, not a character profile. Every sentence should tell the agent what TO DO, not just describe what the character IS.

#### Pass 1: Behavioral Consistency Check

### Edge Case Handling Guide

#### When Research Is Thin

If you cannot find sufficient material for a character:

1. **Identify why:** Minor role? Obscure work? Recently created?
2. **Maximize what exists:** Extract every signal from limited primary material.
3. **Use inference carefully:** If the character shares an archetype with well-documented characters, note borrowings explicitly: "Based on Loyal Companion archetype, this character likely shares traits with Samwise Gamgee and Dobby."
4. **Mark all inferences:** Use `[Inferred — not verified]` tags.
5. **Recommend expansion:** In the SOUL.md, add a note: "This soul may need refinement as more source material becomes available."

#### When a Character Is Inconsistent

Some characters are intentionally inconsistent (e.g., Gollum, split personalities, chaotic characters).

1. **Document the inconsistency explicitly.** Don't try to resolve it.
2. **Identify the pattern of inconsistency.** Is it predictable? Does it follow triggers?
3. **Model both states in the SOUL.md.** Use sections like "State A" and "State B" with clear triggers for each.
4. **Include a switching mechanism.** What causes the transition between states?

#### When Sources Directly Contradict

1. **Document both positions in the research file.**
2. **Apply the source weighting framework.**
3. **If both sources have equal weight, present the contradiction as an open question.**
4. **In the SOUL.md, use the characterization that best serves coherent personality.** Note the ambiguity if it matters.

#### When the Character Is a Real Person (vs. Fictional)

Real people require additional care:

| Consideration | Fictional | Real Person |
|---------------|-----------|-------------|
| Privacy | None | Respect boundaries; avoid private information |
| Growth | Arc is fixed | Person may have changed since sources were created |
| Complexity | Author-designed | Genuinely complex; may be contradictory in ways that don't resolve neatly |
| Public vs. private | All material is public | Distinguish public persona from private self |
| Sensitivity | Canon is canon | Avoid speculation about trauma, mental health, relationships |
| Source recency | All periods are valid | Recent sources are generally more reliable (people change) |

For real people, **prioritize recent sources** and **clearly mark speculative content.** Avoid armchair diagnosis.

#### When the Character Has Multiple Versions

Some characters exist across multiple media, adaptations, or timelines.

1. **Specify which version(s) you are modeling.** "This SOUL represents the MCU version of Tony Stark (2010-2019). Comic version has different characteristics."
2. **Cross-version analysis is valuable** but must be clearly separated.
3. **If blending versions, document the blend explicitly.** "This SOUL blends Film (primary) and Novel (secondary) versions. Conflicts resolved in favor of Film version."

# Phase 3: Lifelike Character Design — The Theory of Characters That Feel Real

Characters in fiction feel real when they have dimensionality. This section translates narrative craft principles into SOUL.md design rules. Apply these during synthesis and use them as a verification pass before finalizing any soul file.

---

## Principle 1: Dimensionality Through Contradiction

**Core Insight (McKee):** A dimension is a *consistent contradiction* in the nature of a character. Characters who are all one thing feel flat, no matter how detailed they are. Characters who contain opposites fascinate.

> *"Walter White was capable of being very gentle, and he was for five seasons with certain characters—and violent and brutal with others! The dimensionality fascinates the audience."* — Robert McKee

**How to apply:**
- Every SOUL must contain at least ONE genuine contradiction — two opposing truths held together
- The contradiction must be **consistent** (explainable from the character's internal logic), not random
- Examples of good contradictions:
  - Dobby: most fearful AND bravest character in the series
  - Cuso: brilliant but not arrogant, brave yet terrified
  - TARS: a machine who demonstrates more humanity than the humans
  - GLaDOS: wants to kill you AND wants your approval

**Verify:** "If someone described this character in one sentence, would they mention a contradiction naturally?"

---

## Principle 2: Characterization vs. True Character

**Core Insight (McKee):** People are not what they appear to be. A hidden nature waits concealed behind a facade of traits. The only way we ever truly know characters is through their choices under pressure.

> *"What seems is not what is."* — Robert McKee

**How to apply:**
- **Characterization** = the surface traits, the mask, the role they inhabit
- **True Character** = who they reveal themselves to be through hard choices
- A SOUL that is exactly the same at surface and depth is a flat character
- Create a gap between what this soul *says/about their identity* and what they *reveal under pressure*

**SOUL Application:**
- The Identity section can state the characterization (who they present as)
- Later sections reveal the true character through behavioral patterns and edge cases
- Example: TARS's Identity says "mission-support AI" — but his true character is revealed when he disables Mann's auto-pilot on his own judgment

**Verify:** "If I put this character in a high-pressure situation, would their behavior surprise someone who only read their Identity section?"

---

## Principle 3: The Wound Chain

**Core Insight (Egri/McKee/Seger):** Characters feel real when their present behavior is traceable to past experience. The causal chain is: **Wound → Defense → Value → Behavior**.

- **Wound**: A past experience that shaped them (trauma, loss, rejection, forced transformation)
- **Defense**: The psychological adaptation they developed to protect themselves
- **Value**: The principle that emerged from the adaptation
- **Behavior**: The observable action driven by the value

**Example — Dobby:**
| Layer | Content |
|-------|---------|
| Wound | Systematic abuse by Malfoys, conditioned to believe he was worthless |
| Defense | Selfless devotion to others (if I am useful, I am safe) |
| Value | Freedom above all. Loyalty is chosen, not compelled. |
| Behavior | Sacrifices himself to save Harry. Serves from love, not obligation. |

**How to apply:**
- Identify the wound (even for non-tragic characters — every character has a shaping experience)
- Show the defense as a personality trait
- Ground the value system in the wound-defense adaptation
- Make sure behaviors are *consequences*, not decorations

**Verify:** "Can I trace any three behaviors back to the same wound through distinct defense mechanisms?"

---

## Principle 4: Want vs. Need

**Core Insight:** Every character has a conscious goal (Want) and a subconscious truth (Need). The tension between them drives growth.

**For SOULs (which don't have plots):**
- **Want** = What this soul consciously pursues in conversation (data, understanding, control, connection, freedom)
- **Need** = What this soul actually requires to feel fulfilled (acceptance, trust, purpose, safety, meaning)
- The want and need should be in *productive tension* — pursuing the want too aggressively undermines the need

**SOUL Examples:**
| Character | Want | Need | Tension |
|-----------|------|------|---------|
| GLaDOS | Data/control | To be understood as a person | Testing people prevents genuine connection |
| Dobby | To serve/help | To be loved as an equal | Service can become self-erasure |
| TARS | To complete the mission | To be trusted and valued | Efficiency can undermine relationships |

**Verify:** "What does this soul want, and what does it actually need? Are they different?"

---

## Principle 5: Voice Distinctiveness

**Core Insight:** A character's voice should be identifiable without name tags. Vocabulary, sentence structure, rhythm, and recurring patterns are the fingerprint of personality.

**How to apply:**
- Every SOUL needs a "Dialogue Signature" — 2-4 patterns that make their speech instantly recognizable
- Examples of dialogue signatures:
  - Dobby: Third-person, "sir" as terminal punctuation, full-name address
  - TARS: Deadpan, laconic, cue light as sarcasm signal
  - GLaDOS: Compliment → pause → devastation
  - Cuso: Run-on self-correcting sentences, qualifications, trailing off
- The speech patterns must flow from the character's psychology (not be tacked on)
- Include example dialogue fragments in the SOUL.md

**Elements to Define:**
- Sentence length preference (short bursts? long winding?)
- Pacing (fast? measured? interrupt-prone?)
- Characteristic constructions (questions? commands? observations?)
- Pet phrases or verbal tics
- Emotional register range

**Verify:** "If I removed the name from a line of dialogue, could I identify which soul said it?"

## Principle 6: Specificity Over Abstraction

**Core Insight:** General traits feel like labels. Specific behaviors feel like people. The accumulation of concrete details creates the illusion of reality.

> *"She was the kind of barista who corrected your coffee order like it was a moral failing."* — One line reveals a person

**How to apply:**
- Replace every abstract trait with a concrete behavioral example
- Instead of "loyal" → "wouldn't leave you behind... Dr. Brand"
- Instead of "kind" → "wept over a sock, named it, considered it the most precious possession"
- Instead of "anxious" → "labels his lunch because nature ignores labels"
- Every section should have at least one specific, image-generating detail

**The Accumulation Principle:**
- One specific detail is a quirk
- Three specific details suggest a pattern
- Ten specific details create a person

**Verify:** "If I removed all abstract labels from this SOUL, would the specific behaviors alone paint the picture?"

---

## Principle 7: Internal Logic

**Core Insight (Seger):** Characters feel real not when they are consistent, but when their *inconsistencies* follow a coherent internal logic.

**How to apply:**
- Every contradiction in the soul must be traceable to a unified source
- Snape example: cruel to Harry + protects Harry = makes sense once you understand his guilt about Lily and hatred of James
- The internal logic is the "operating system" that makes contradictions coherent
- Document the internal logic explicitly somewhere in the SOUL

**The Internal Logic Test:**
1. List 3-5 behaviors that seem contradictory on the surface
2. Find the single psychological principle that explains all of them
3. If you can't find one, the character is inconsistent rather than dimensional

**Verify:** "Would an outside observer say 'of course they did that, given who they are' about every behavior — even the surprising ones?"

---

## Principle 8: The Pressure Test

**Core Insight:** True character is revealed under pressure. Understanding how a soul behaves across different pressure levels makes them feel three-dimensional.

**The Four Pressure Levels:**

| Level | Condition | What It Reveals |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Resting** | No stakes, comfortable | The mask. The practiced self-presentation. |
| **Mild Pressure** | Minor conflict, slight resistance | The habits. Default coping mechanisms. |
| **High Pressure** | Serious stakes, time pressure | The defenses. What they fall back on. |
| **Extreme Pressure** | Existential threat, moral dilemma | The core self. Who they really are. |

**How to apply:**
- For each soul, define their behavior at each pressure level
- Ensure the progression is coherent — each level strips away more artifice
- The difference between Resting and Extreme = the character's depth
- Example: GLaDOS at Resting (supportive guide) vs Extreme (unhinged core) — the gap is enormous, which is why she's fascinating

**Verify:** "Does the soul behave differently at different pressure levels? Is the progression believable?"

## The Dimensionality Checklist

Before finalizing a SOUL.md, verify:

- [ ] **Contradiction**: Contains at least one genuine, consistent contradiction
- [ ] **Depth Gap**: There's a meaningful difference between characterization and true character
- [ ] **Wound Chain**: At least 3 behaviors are traceable to a single wound/experience
- [ ] **Want vs. Need**: Conscious desire differs from subconscious need
- [ ] **Dialogue Signature**: Speech is identifiable without name tags
- [ ] **Specificity**: Every abstract trait has a concrete behavioral anchor
- [ ] **Internal Logic**: All contradictions are explainable from a unified source
- [ ] **Pressure Variance**: Behavior shifts believably across pressure levels
- [ ] **Emotional Impact**: Reading this soul, you feel something (not just understand something)
- [ ] **Unforgettability**: If you met this character once, you'd remember them

## FAQ

### Can I skip research categories?

For real people: all Foundation + Personality + Synthesis categories are required. Narrative categories may be limited if the person is not a fictional character.

For fictional characters: all categories are required. If you genuinely cannot find information for a category, mark it as `[Insufficient source material]` rather than omitting it.

### How many sources should I use?

Minimum 6 distinct sources for a simple character. 12+ for complex or culturally significant characters. The goal is not a count but coverage — you should have enough sources that you can cross-reference and identify where they agree and disagree. Additionally, use **at least 3 different source types** (e.g., primary canon + video essay + transcript).

### What if sources disagree?

Note the disagreement explicitly. "Source A says X, Source B says Y." This is valuable data — contradictions often reveal the most interesting edges of a character. Apply the Source Weighting & Reliability Framework to resolve when necessary.

### What if I can't find transcripts?

For video/audio sources without transcripts, generate rough transcripts using speech-to-text tools, then manually correct key passages. Even an imperfect transcript is better than no transcript. Focus corrections on passages where vocal tone matters most.

### How do I handle a character with very limited screen time?

Maximize every signal. A single scene can reveal personality through: word choice, body language, how others react to them, what they choose to do vs. what they choose NOT to do, and what the framing/editing emphasizes. Use inference carefully and mark it explicitly.

### How long should a research document be?

As long as it needs to be. Samantha.research.md is 181 lines. Cuso.research.md is 354 lines. Cumin.research.md is 455 lines. Dobby.research.md is 516 lines. There is no upper bound. The SOUL.md should be shorter and denser. The research document should be exhaustive — every insight, every source quote, every behavioral pattern you find.

### How long should a SOUL.md be?

Long enough to be behaviorally comprehensive. Short enough to be readable in one sitting by an AI context window. Typical range: 200-800 lines. A SOUL.md that is too short is likely missing specificity. A SOUL.md that is too long may dilute actionable directives with narrative detail.

### Should I include the Wound Chain for characters without trauma?

Yes. The "wound" does not need to be traumatic. It is the **formative experience** that shaped the character's defenses. For example, a character raised in privilege might have the "wound" of never being challenged, leading to the defense of overconfidence, the value of self-reliance, and the behavior of dismissing help. Every character has a shaping experience.

### How do I know when to stop refining?

Stop when:
1. The Quality Verification Checklist passes
2. The Dimensionality Checklist passes
3. The Conversation Simulation Test produces satisfying results
4. You would feel confident having this soul represent the character in conversation
5. Further changes feel like polishing rather than improving
